PhD Dissertation Research

Overview

Cenozoic climate change drove faunal turnover not only on land, but also in the sea. Along with many environmental changes witnessed in the Cenozoic, the late Pliocene (Piacenzian, 3.6-2.58Ma) warming is regarded as a good analogue for modern climate change. The Piacenzian warming has been cited by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change as “the most recent interval of geological time when mean global temperatures were ~2°C to 3°C above pre-industrial temperatures for a sustained period” and are therefore comparable to those temperatures projected to occur within the next fifty years. By the late Pliocene, continents and ocean basins were already in their present geographic configuration and atmospheric CO2 concentrations (360 - 400 ppm) were at the same level as the modern. The Pliocene, therefore, offers a unique chance to examine the response of biotic systems to a similar warming as that predicted for the future. Coastal ecosystems are highly productive as well as rich in biodiversity and economic resources. These ecosystems have experienced more severe environmental changes with modern global warming as compared to open ocean systems and are critical to study in the fossil record in order to establish a guideline for future environmental and ecological change.

Mollusks are a particularly important study group within these ecosystems due to their taphonomic durability, abundance in the fossil record, and habitat suitability which is strongly dependent on multiple ecological factors such as productivity, temperature, and salinity. In the right taphonomic and stratigraphic circumstances, the molluskan fossil record allows for the construction of time-series of community structure. Mollusk shells can also be used to construct time series of environmental change as stable isotopes are incorporated into molluskan shell microstructure from surrounding seawater, preserving an isotopic signal reflective of the environmental conditions at the time of shell formation. The aforementioned characteristics of mollusks make them the ideal study organism for understanding the response of coastal ecosystems to climate change and the environmental drivers behind that response.

Marine molluskan community responses to Pliocene climate change have been studied in the Caribbean and in New Zealand, showing evidence of extinction and faunal turnover in both the Atlantic and western Pacific in response to warming. The coast of the northeastern Pacific still remains a gap in marine Pliocene paleoecological and paleoenvironmental studies. While the immigration of warm water taxa into Californian waters during the Pliocene has been reported, no studies have offered a quantitative analysis of community structure change over the late Pliocene warming. Furthermore, no studies have attempted to include paleoenvironmental analyses to provide context to observed ecological changes.

My dissertation focuses on analyzing the response of marine mollusk communities to late Pliocene warming down the coast of California and determining the environmental drivers behind observed responses using stable isotopic analyses.
