PhD Dissertation Research

Overview

Cenozoic climate change drove faunal turnover not only on land, but also in the sea. Along with many environmental changes witnessed in the Cenozoic, the late Pliocene (Piacenzian, 3.6-2.58Ma) warming is regarded as a good analogue for modern climate change. The Piacenzian warming has been cited by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change as “the most recent interval of geological time when mean global temperatures were ~2°C to 3°C above pre-industrial temperatures for a sustained period” and are therefore comparable to those temperatures projected to occur within the next fifty years. By the late Pliocene, continents and ocean basins were already in their present geographic configuration and atmospheric CO2 concentrations (360 - 400 ppm) were at the same level as the modern. The Pliocene, therefore, offers a unique chance to examine the response of biotic systems to a similar warming as that predicted for the future. Coastal ecosystems are highly productive as well as rich in biodiversity and economic resources. These ecosystems have experienced more severe environmental changes with modern global warming as compared to open ocean systems and are critical to study in the fossil record in order to establish a guideline for future environmental and ecological change.

Mollusks are a particularly important study group within these ecosystems due to their taphonomic durability, abundance in the fossil record, and habitat suitability which is strongly dependent on multiple ecological factors such as productivity, temperature, and salinity. Using ordination analyses of molluscan fossil assemblages, the environmental drivers shaping faunal distribution can be determined. My dissertation focuses on analyzing the differences in marine mollusk communities preserved within the Plio-Pleistocene Santa Maria Basin Foxen Mudstone and Careaga Sandstone, the Ventura Basin Pico Formation, and the San Diego Basin San Diego Formation of Southern California in order to determine the environmental gradients driving community differences within and between geological formations during a period of climate change.

Content Chapters

  1. On the Reconciliation of Biostratigraphy and Strontium Isotope Stratigraphy of Three Southern Californian Plio-Pleistocene Formations

    2. Former studies primarily used macrofossil biostratigraphy to assign ages to the Plio-Pleistocene formations of interest for analysis of environmental gradients. These methods are inaccurate and provided low resolution ages. Higher resolution ages needed to be assigned to formations in order to provide climatic context to all assemblages. Strontium isotope stratigraphy of molluscan carbonate was used to calculate these higher resolution ages.

  2. Reconstructing Marine Paleo-Temperatures Using Mollusks: A Comparison Between Stable Isotope and Effective Temperature Methods in the Plio-Pleistocene Santa Maria Basin, California

    3. The effective temperature method for paleothermometry was compared to the stable isotope geochemistry method for paleothermometry. This was necessary to determine whether ordination plots could be coded using the effective temperatures of localities, a necessary step in determining if paleotemperature was important environmental gradient driving faunal distribution.

  3. Environmental Drivers of Faunal Change Over Space and Time in the Plio-Pleistocene Paleo-Californian Molluscan Province

    4. While previous studies on the formations of interest used faunal surveys of fossil molluscan assemblages, no previous studies used quantitative analytical methods to determine the environmental gradients driving faunal distribution over time and space. Python was used for data cleaning, analysis, and data visualization for this chapter. Reduced dimension ordination analyses were used to determine environmental gradients and clustering of localities.
